  • So we're going to start by covering the 2 points which acted as the precursor for us as a team and our use of polymer to deliver projects.
  • Hopefully this way you’ll get a sense of the environment we built as well as the technical approach.

1. collaboration

  • A couple of years ago Net-A-Porter made its move to form cross-compentency teams.
  • Teams made of design / product owner / engineers and testers. Creating small, focused product teams.

empathy

  • There’s a lot of writings about the benefits of multi functional teams, and this isn’t anything new so we won’t go on about it.
  • But the key for us as a team was this new ability to listen and learn - making us able to pivot and accelerate through change.
  • Maturing our inter team processes over time has gave us the platform to;
  • Constant conversation through to live
  • Remove any Hand-offs and bring development forwards into design - making smarter decisions
  • Remove any Hand-offs and bring development forwards into design - making smarter decisions
  • Understand the pain points
  • Move forwards together

challenge

  • As a team our thinking became more matured and as such the team led by the designers started to challenge the design approach used at NAP.
  • What was once a process driven by showcasing full formed pages designs at the end of two week sprints - seemed increasingly outdated, and began to derail our agility.

Samantha Warren

@samanthatoy
  • A talk by Jeremy Keith from Clearleft followed by one by Samantha Warren when she was at Twitter, accelerated our conceptual thinking and experience approach. and brought us to idea of Styletil.es showcasing elements in granularity
  • We developed that into a concept that would work with our company, showcasing early with granular elements and working against design hypothesis created in collaboration with our stakeholders Phraseology coined by Brad Frost - 'Atomic design', rapidly changed the lexicon around design at NAP.

learn

  • As with any change there are also pitfalls and one of those was to try to implement a working component library on an ageing web app.
  • Primarily because there was huge maintain overheads, poor communication and high time investment to implement styles across the whole app.

review

  • But with every time we fail it give us a time to be retrospective. And this was no different - the result was we open conversations about connecting strategies design and development.
  • This is really a critical understanding - design and technology strategy can’t succeed without the other.
  • We had to identity an opportunity to collaborate and deliver working components libraries at the same time.

2. opportunity

  • Our opportunity to deliver came when a piece of internal research highlighted a potential missed opportunity. Driven by the conscious realisation that our key competitors were beginning to deliver faster, scalable sites
  • We had a fixed container width at 950px and a separate mobile UI and rarely releasing features across both.
  • Through data we saw that our consistently most profitable browser width was 1300-1400. This Paralleled with the trending change in traffic entry points from our visitors, and big growth in the APAC region from mobile devices.

question

  • So the questions asked of us was “how can we create a proposition that recaptures the market, without alienating our core customer base during the process?"
  • We won’t go through all of the steps between this question being asked of us and the beginning of the development. But a couple of crucial decisions were made in the interim
  • This had to be an iterative release
  • We needed to split away from our current web app
  • we needed to showcase early to our stakeholders
  • we needed to showcase early to our stakeholders
  • We started our responsive journey with our global header and footer - deciding to create a responsive site framework first before focusing on our content pages.

the turning point

  • Attended Google I/O - heard about Polymer 1.0
  • Tried a basic component - easy to get started
  • But was this going to work in production?
  • Browser support currently poor
  • Image = aggrgation of all 4 standards needed for Web Components
  • Chrome fine, Firefox and Edge getting there. IE never, Safari??

risk vs reward

  • W3 Standard - soon no Polyfill needed
  • Not a framework - can pick and choose when you want to use it.
  • Feels like Web dev, no framework in the way
  • Speed to development high.
